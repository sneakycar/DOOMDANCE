# The Founding Ledger

## Volume I — Early Marks and Permanent Names

*The origins of the MakeAwesome League and the people who wrote its first chapters*

**Compiled for the MAL Founding Archive**  
*Gaylord Heynen*

---

## Prologue — One Standings Page

The MakeAwesome League did not begin as a dream. It began as paperwork.

Every baseball league lies about this eventually. The official photographs always arrive later: men in dark coats shaking hands near a rail siding, somebody holding a hat against the wind, everybody pretending the sport had been waiting patiently for them to invent it. The MAL kept those photographs too. Somewhere in the archive there is still a merger story about rival associations realizing the public would tolerate one standings page but not two. Another version blames winter lawsuits and radio money. A third insists the league was stitched together by train schedules because nobody trusted the highways yet. All of those stories survive in the file. None of them fully agree.

What survives more clearly is the paperwork itself.

The Founding Archive sits in the league’s imagination the way a courthouse sits in an old county seat: not always visited, never entirely ignored. Neutral-site games were played there when two cities could not agree whose foul pole was honest.

Attendance extremes were filed there—eighty-eight thousand once, in a year when the league still believed crowds mattered more than television contracts; one hundred ninety-nine another time, on a Tuesday when the wind off the water made the ushers apologize to arriving fans before they even sat down.

Postponements. Wire volume. Doubleheaders. Seventeen delayed games during the flood season of 2024. The archive keeps records that do not win arguments in bars but do occasionally end arguments in offices.

If Cooperstown is where baseball goes to be admired, the Founding Archive is where baseball goes to be corrected.

I have spent years in that building, or what passes for it in this book: a room of ledgers, warped drawers, yellowing score sheets, microfilm that smells faintly of vinegar and dust warmed by old lights. The league presents itself to children as a chain of summers. The archive presents it as revisions. Sometimes the revisions improve the story. Sometimes they reduce it to weather reports and train delays.

That gap may be the sport.

Baseball, at its honestest, belongs to two kinds of people: the dream-keepers and the record-keepers. The dream-keepers sell the rookie who looks like redemption, the father in the stands, the sunset behind the scoreboard. The record-keepers sell the line score and the attendance count and the protest ruling filed three weeks later. The MakeAwesome League has always needed both, though for most of its history it has trusted the second group more.

A respectable record book is a kind of civic architecture. It tells a city that its afternoons counted for something even when the factory whistles drowned out the radio call. The MAL record book is respectable until you start reading the footnotes. Then it becomes human.

The footnotes are why this volume exists.

For orientation, the archive sorts its own mythology into eras the way other leagues sort decades:

- **Foundry and Radio** — industrial cities, missing schedules, *radio_only* marks
- **Merger** — continental clubs, *pre_merger* tags, translation problems
- **Blackout** — missing footage, negative records kept with equal care
- **Modern Audit** — OPS, WAR, *office-qualified* lines, Comet Season

Volume I is not a complete accounting of the permanent file. God help the reader who wants that. The archive holds decades of official lines, disputed lines, corrected lines, radio-only lines, and weather-damaged lines. Every one of them has a clerk attached to it somewhere. This book is narrower. It concerns the early league—the foundry cities, the merger clubs, the blackout years, the radio voices, the first legends whose names hardened into league property before anyone fully understood what the league was becoming.

You will not meet all one hundred fifty Hall of Famers here. The wall at the Founding Archive holds one hundred fifty plaques because every league eventually decides immortality looks better in rows. Thirty-two anchors sit at the center—the names children learn first. One hundred eighteen outer rows fill the rest from the historical pools through 2026. The math is simple on purpose: thirty-two plus one hundred eighteen equals one wall.

The story begins earlier than induction ceremonies. It begins when the league was still inventing the sound of its own voice—industrial, defensive, suspicious of spectacle unless the attendance justified it. It begins in Gary and Doon and Tonopah. It begins during the radio years when a home run could become real because a broadcaster insisted it was real loudly enough for long enough.

It begins with Nolan Creed winning twenty-nine games in a summer nobody can fully reconstruct. It begins with Denny Root collecting seven hits in a game local papers called indecently long. It begins with Len Casket and the four home runs that belong to Tonopah Else in one archive drawer and to argument everywhere else.

I am not writing to settle those arguments. The archive does not settle arguments. It stores them alphabetically.

I am writing because a league that asks people to care about fictional careers on a telephone eventually owes them some older ghosts. The MakeAwesome League is not real the way Cincinnati is real. It is real the way a permanent file is real: maintained, corrected, coffee-stained, occasionally contradictory, and strangely insulted when somebody questions the handwriting.

So: one standings page, two merger stories, and a room where the sport learned to count before it learned to explain itself.

Put on the bad light. The foundries are still warm.

## Chapter 1 — Foundry and Radio

The Foundry Era sounds romantic until you stand in Gary in April.

Gary Foundry was not named as a joke, though the league spent decades treating it like one. The club came out of the same industrial instinct that produced half the early league: cities convinced a box score could substitute for a skyline while the skyline itself was occupied with smoke and payroll. The uniforms were gray in a way that photographs still struggle to explain properly. Not clean gray. Furnace gray. The sort of gray that settles into sleeves.

They played baseball the way industrial towns tended to do everything then—hard, tired, occasionally suspicious of enjoyment. Ronnie Flood would later hit seventy-one doubles in a single summer, a mark that briefly turned every alley in the league into civic property. That came later. Early Gary mostly specialized in enduring things.

Doon Static arrived in the file as Gary’s cousin in misery, though the league office preferred the term “travel partner.” The map insists they are separate towns. The weather insists otherwise. In the Foundry years, Doon baseball existed beneath a permanent electrical hum. The lights buzzed. The infield chewed cleats. Local broadcasts faded in and out like conversations overheard through walls.

Historians enjoy pairing Gary and Doon because historians enjoy symmetry. The people living there probably would have preferred victories.

Survival was the early league’s real religion anyway. Winning entered later, after the mergers and the television years and the discovery that dynasties sold merchandise better than competence did.

Nolan Creed pitched for Gary Foundry when pitching still carried moral overtones in newspaper writing. He won twenty-nine games in 1973. The number remains official. The schedule around it does not.

This bothered people immediately.

The archive contains surviving win columns, partial schedules, radio recaps, travel ledgers, and at least three contradictory versions of a road trip through Ohio. What it does not contain is a complete season reconstruction everyone agrees upon. Vernon Latch once told a radio host that Creed’s twenty-nine wins marked the first time he realized MAL baseball was being governed by filing cabinets instead of opponents. Latch spent the rest of his life saying versions of that sentence to reporters.

He never managed a winning season.

The archive liked him anyway.

If Creed taught the league that official numbers could survive missing evidence, Denny Root taught it that evidence sometimes survives without attracting much mythology around it. Root played second base for Mesa Neon and collected seven hits in a single game in 1988. The line score survives cleanly. The newspaper stories survive less cleanly. One paper called the game “indecent in length.” Another blamed relief pitching. A third spent most of the article complaining about parking near the stadium.

That last clipping survives in the archive too.

Seven hits is less romantic in person than it sounds in a record book. It means somebody kept reaching base while everyone else slowly became exhausted. It means scorers sharpening pencils in the eighth inning. It means the visiting club missing its train home.

The archive preserves all of it with the same seriousness.

Then there is Len Casket.

Tonopah Else sits in the file like a rumor the league accidentally incorporated. Thin air. Dust. Road trips nobody wanted. Casket played right field there during the Radio Era, when broadcasts occasionally carried more authority than the box scores themselves because radio at least sounded confident.

The broadcast insists he hit four home runs in one game.

The provenance tag attached to the mark reads *radio_only*, which is one of the archive’s great euphemisms. It means there are witnesses but not enough paperwork. I have listened to fragments of the surviving tape more than once. You can still hear the crowd swelling before the announcer finishes his sentences. On the fourth home run call, the broadcaster sounds briefly offended by his own excitement.

Whether Casket actually hit four home runs matters less now than the fact that the league allowed the argument to survive intact. That may be the league’s healthiest instinct historically: resisting the urge to clean itself up completely.

Creed belonged to the win column. Root belonged to the scorer’s table. Casket belonged to the radio speaker in the dashboard of a parked car outside a factory gate.

Around them drifted the clerks and half-officials who always seem to populate early baseball history. Vernon Latch voting for Hall of Fame candidates despite never managing a winner. Marta Glass keeping duplicate box scores in desk drawers because she distrusted the copies upstairs. The Red Directory before it became institutional mythology—back then it was mostly a color and a rumor about corrections happening in basement offices after midnight.

The cities changed slower than the stories did. Tonopah. Rock Island. Gary. Doon. Places that appear in the extension tier of the founding record book like towns in the index of a forgotten novel.

The Foundry Era left the league with habits it never entirely abandoned. Keep the mark. Keep the argument nearby. Keep the footnote attached even when the league would prefer cleaner mythology.

Gary Foundry later endured a twenty-four-game losing streak in 1978. Doon Static lost one hundred nineteen games in 2001—a season the schedule printed anyway. Alaska Avalanche won one hundred seven in 2024 and somehow made absurdity feel optimistic instead of embarrassing. The league kept all three records with identical seriousness.

The Foundry and Radio years were still young enough to believe one standings page could contain the sport. The standings page survived. Everything else spilled into the margins.

That spillover became the real history.

The foundries cooled. The league was about to learn that darkness could be international too.

## Chapter 2 — Merger and Blackout

The Merger Era did not feel like expansion. It felt administrative.

The league had spent years convincing itself the Foundry cities were the center of baseball reality. Then Cairo Stars arrived with different weather and different crowds. Sapporo Heat brought humidity into the box scores. Veracruz Sons played afternoon games that made eastern scorers complain about exhaustion in language normally reserved for wars and roofing jobs.

San Juan Saints brought noise the old industrial clubs distrusted immediately.

The office called it growth.

The archive mostly called it more folders.

Merger baseball forced the league to translate itself. A home run in Gary sounded like labor. A home run in Cairo sounded argumentative. The Founding Archive started attaching *pre_merger* tags to older marks from the absorbed associations because clerks in Erie and Dayton kept questioning numbers from Veracruz the way older men question new street layouts.

Nobody admits this publicly now. The paperwork survives anyway.

Nico Velvet played left field for Cairo during the Wall-Move summer of 1976, which remains one of the most embarrassing episodes the league has ever successfully preserved. Somebody measured the outfield incorrectly. The wall moved in July. Velvet finished with fifty-four home runs and a permanent place in every future argument about geometry and honesty.

The archive keeps the mark official because there is no practical alternative.

Mack Liturgy played first base for Cairo later and hit four hundred twenty-one career home runs across seasons interrupted by travel restrictions, labor pauses, and at least one year where scheduling resembled diplomacy more than sport. Liturgy belongs to the merger era because his career looks multinational even when the statistics try to simplify him into a slugger.

Then the lights started failing.

Nobody fully agrees on when the Blackout Era begins. The archive does not seem interested in deciding. It only needed a drawer label broad enough to hold missing footage, damaged attendance sheets, interrupted broadcasts, and games where the bat sounded different because everyone in the stadium was waiting for darkness.

Marta Glass handled much of the blackout filing by then. She had stopped being a young clerk years earlier. Her name appears in enough correction logs from the period that retired archivists still refer to certain folders simply as “Glass work.”

The league survived those years through repetition more than confidence. Games kept starting. Clerks kept filing them. The standings kept appearing in newspapers the next morning whether the lighting systems cooperated or not.

Mack Bellweather slugged .711 for Akron Static in 2002. People who attended those games still insist the ball sounded harder off his bat after partial outages. People who were not there usually blame memory. The archive keeps the mark and leaves the explanation section mostly empty.

That restraint may be healthier than certainty.

Rocco Dream hit five home runs in a game for Biloxi Radios in 1997. The fifth home run still lacks verified footage. This has not prevented the league from promoting the game repeatedly over the years, though promotional departments tend to become quieter whenever archivists enter the room.

Five home runs creates practical problems. Four can survive as folklore. Five forces somebody to explain where the camera operators were.

The archive eventually stopped trying.

Negative records accumulated during the blackout years too, and the league preserves them with almost suspicious care. Hector Drain lost thirty-one games for Norfolk Flood in 1996 while attendance remained surprisingly stable. Willow Bent appeared in one hundred sixty-four games in 1991 because replay protests and rescheduled doubleheaders kept generating baseball faster than the roster could absorb it. Bret Window committed five errors for Sioux Falls Steam in what later became known as the municipal lighting apology game, which sounds fictional even inside the archive itself.

Roman Nail struck out nineteen for Akron Owls in 1977 on a night train delays swallowed most of the film crews. Earl Cinder won twenty-six games for Dayton Miners in 1982 despite six disputed starts still attached to the record decades later. None of these marks entirely belong to greatness. Many belong to endurance instead.

The blackout years taught the league two habits it never abandoned.

First: attendance and embarrassment are perfectly capable of coexisting.

Second: a disputed record can still become permanent if enough paperwork survives around it.

Marta Glass understood that before most people did. She filed the games. She filed the missing footage reports. She filed the coffee stains and replacement pages and weather addendums. The archive learned from her that preservation does not require certainty.

The Red Directory was changing too. It stopped behaving like gossip and started behaving like infrastructure. Corrections became procedural. Anonymous. Slightly feared. Junie Knife reportedly kept a glove in the office because neutral-site representatives were still expected to resemble athletes from a distance during photographs.

Somebody probably invented that story later. The archive kept it anyway.

By the end of the merger and blackout years, the MakeAwesome League had stopped trying to appear orderly. Continental mattered more than orderly now. Strange mattered more too.

A child in Anchorage was about to learn a name before learning a city.

The archive was ready for that.

Mostly.

## Chapter 3 — The Modern Audit

The Modern Audit Era begins when the league stops pretending the numbers are shy.

For decades the archive had survived on arguments: radio against pencil, scorer against film, wall distance against common sense. The modern audit clerks did not end disagreement. They standardized its vocabulary. OPS. OPS+. WAR. Acronyms that fit on a form and still leave room for a clerk to disagree over lunch. The reform did not make baseball more honest. It made honesty easier to file.

The audit clerks think in pairs. They always have. Alaska’s one hundred seven wins in 2024 and Doon Static’s one hundred nineteen losses in 2001 belong in the same weather system even though twenty-three years separate them—glacier and sinkhole, optimism and static, the league’s appetite for magnitude pointed in opposite directions and filed with identical seriousness.

I start with Doon because misery is easier to understand before you try to understand glory.

In 2001 the Static lost one hundred nineteen times. The schedule was printed anyway. Attendance clerks stopped making eye contact. Kerry Box drove in twelve runs in a game that same era; the scoreboard operator quit afterward, according to the clipping. The franchise became a punchline so durable that modern audit meetings still invoke the season the way sailors invoke a shipwreck—not because anyone enjoys it, but because everyone agrees where the bottom is.

Then Milo Castor collected two hundred eleven hits for Doon Static in 2023.

The number arrived without ceremony. No radio-only tag. No missing footage report. Just a shortstop wearing static gray in a city that had already been laughed at for a generation, reaching base often enough that the league eventually stopped pretending the mark was a typo. Castor did not save Doon. He did not redeem the franchise in any narrative sense the promotional office could use. He simply proved that misery and emptiness are not the same condition historically. A city can lose one hundred nineteen and still produce two hundred eleven. The archive needed that lesson before it could accept what came next.

What came next was Comet Season.

The archive uses the name for the summer of 2024 because nobody found a better phrase in time and because comets, seen once, pass for birds in memory if enough people look at the sky at once. The season looked ordinary while it was happening. That is probably why the league trusts it. Ordinary is the only way to keep a file from catching fire.

Alaska Avalanche won one hundred seven games that year. The line sits in the team record with the calm confidence of a glacier on a map—enormous, slow, obvious after the fact. Orson Vale hit sixty-one home runs for the same club. Sixty-one is not the clean church number from the other league the MAL office pretends not to watch. It is sixty-one, which means somebody had to say it aloud in a meeting and live with the minutes.

I was not in that meeting. The surviving notes suggest the room went quiet in the bureaucratic way rooms go quiet when everyone realizes the number is already written somewhere and cannot be politely unwritten.

Vale belongs to the founding book whether your franchise memory begins this decade or in the last century. Some names the office does not shuffle. His sixty-one is orientation: a child entering the league for the first time arrives in a world where sixty-one already happened, fixed in place before her own countdown timer starts.

Marcus Wren played left field for Fresno Floodlights the same summer and posted a 1.121 OPS. Wren’s season is what Comet Season feels like when you read it alone in the file at night—not fraudulent, just unstable, the way certain summers become unstable after enough people remember them simultaneously. The mark still makes clerks nervous. Not because they doubt it. Because doubt and certification are different departments, and both departments signed the form.

Otis Mercy reads retired box scores the way some people read cemetery inscriptions. Patiently. He rarely appears in the visible record outside small notes and correction initials, but his handwriting is all over the modern audit section. At two in the morning during Comet Season he left a margin note defending a typo correction in Wren’s file because he believed future clerks would otherwise distrust the whole summer unnecessarily.

That kind of thinking built the modern archive.

Kenji Small hit .371 for Gary Foundry in 2024 and qualified for the batting title through office ruling. Small’s average survives as both achievement and legal territory—glory with a footnote that cites jurisdiction. The Red Directory had stopped behaving like a rumor by then. Corrections filed overnight. Appeals in the morning. Revised lines before dinner. The league learned to say *office-qualified* with the same exhausted tone other leagues reserve for injury reports.

Not every summer in the audit drawer rises to Comet Season. The extension tier holds the rest.

Huntington Beach drew nearly three million seven hundred thousand fans in 2016—municipal evidence rather than spectacle. Portland Fog averaged forty-nine thousand a night in 1995 because Tuesdays are difficult. Benny Mercy drove in one hundred forty-one for Akron Telegraph in 2019; Orlando Cuff reached base often enough to irritate pitchers; Damon Phelps posted a 233 OPS+ season in 1994 the office stamped twice. Cal Root’s strikeouts sit permanently in Portland gray. Ronnie Kettle saved five hundred eleven games for Long Beach Sirens to the same walk-up song until repetition itself became historical texture. Duke Warden struck out twenty-one for Doon Static on a night one stadium light failed—the broken fixture preserved in metadata because the archive dislikes throwing anything away once paperwork has acknowledged it.

The largest change in the audit years was not statistical. It was psychological. The office became less embarrassed by its own authority.

When I walk the modern audit section, I notice the cities first—Anchorage, Fresno, Gary, Huntington Beach, Portland—and then the neutral-site records at the Founding Archive itself: eighty-eight thousand once, one hundred ninety-nine once, seventeen postponed games in 2024 because weather and scheduling still refuse to cooperate with mythology.

The league does not overcome the world. It files the world.

The paperwork became confident. The arguments stayed. That, more than any single summer, is what the Modern Audit Era gave the MakeAwesome League.

## Chapter 4 — The Numbers Every Child Learns

There is a difference between a record and a name.

A record is an occurrence. It happened, or it happened enough, or it happened in a language the office accepts. A name is a habit. You say it before you know why. The MakeAwesome League builds its immortality out of names first and numbers second, then pretends it was the other way around because numbers look more serious on a plaque.

The archive knows this. The archive is not above using it.

Harlan Moon played right field for Anchorage Signal from 1997 until his knees stopped cooperating with travel schedules. Four hundred eighty-seven home runs. No provenance tags. No correction notices. No surviving controversy large enough to remain attached permanently. Moon is the first giant most children meet—the league’s ABC, the name that stabilizes the beginning of the conversation before anyone explains what OPS means.

The interviews suggest Moon found the mythology slightly embarrassing. There is a photograph in the extension files where he appears irritated somebody asked him to pose beside a mural of his own swing. The archive preserved the photograph carefully. Moon is useful because he stabilizes. Not every great player stabilizes anything.

Pete Harrow is the name I return to when Moon feels too loud.

Harrow played third base for Cedar Rapids Radios and collected three thousand one hundred eighty-eight hits over a career the historical office marks in ink, as if ink were holiness. Moon feels monumental. Harrow feels unavoidable—the table the comet sets its coffee on. Without Harrow the modern audit era looks like shouting. With him it looks like a language.

Steadiness becomes sacred eventually if it lasts long enough.

Harrow’s file is not dramatic in the way Vale’s file is dramatic. There are no radio-only tags, no Comet Season whispers, no meeting minutes where the room goes quiet. There are innings. There are travel schedules that dissolved into arguments in desert towns. There are summers that looked ordinary while they were happening because ordinary was Harrow’s method. The league did not build curriculum around him the way it built curriculum around Moon. It built trust. Three thousand one hundred eighty-eight is not a fireworks number. It is a civilization number—the kind of total that only exists because somebody kept showing up after the promotional office stopped paying attention.

There is a coffee stain preserved in one of Harrow’s review folders from a clerk who miscounted a double and cried after discovering the mistake. The stain survived three office relocations. Nobody cleaned it. Eventually the folder itself became part of the provenance chain. That is probably more revealing than the hit total. The league talks constantly about records, but records are only the visible layer. What the archive really preserves is accumulation: thousands of ordinary filings hardening into mythology because nobody stopped maintaining them.

The career file holds other names—Owen Chapel, Terry Noll, Ernie Vail, Victor Mercy, Cal Root, Murray Ash, Otis Vale—and the pitchers children learn later, the way children learn vegetables. They are not lesser. They are afternoon class after morning legends. This chapter does not narrate them because narration is not the same as honor. The permanent file honors them whether a compiler does or not.

When people ask why the MakeAwesome League feels real to people who know it is constructed, I return to Harrow’s folder and the coffee ring and the fixed totals beside it.

Your universe shuffles its cities. Your player chases stars on a telephone. The Sickos feed will roast a slump by Friday. But Moon’s four hundred eighty-seven sits where it sat yesterday, and Harrow’s three thousand one hundred eighty-eight sits beside it, and a correction made twenty years ago remains corrected.

The children learn the names first. The clerks keep the names alive afterward. Both jobs matter more than the league admits publicly—and both jobs, if done properly, require the same stubborn affection Harrow’s clerk expressed with tears and coffee and a pencil sharpened twice in the same inning.

## Epilogue — One Hundred Fifty Plaques

The wall at the Founding Archive holds one hundred fifty Hall of Famers: thirty-two anchors and one hundred eighteen outer rows. The anchors are the names children learn first—Moon, Harrow, Vale, Root. The outer plaques arrive later, usually after someone gets curious enough to scroll deeper into the file than the interface really encourages.

Most baseball halls of fame eventually become arguments about standards. The MAL archive solved that problem the way the MAL archive solves most problems: by keeping more paperwork.

The one hundred eighteen outer plaques are filled from the standard historical pools through 2026—good careers, very good careers, careers that lasted long enough for cities to remember the names even after the stadium naming rights changed three times. Some plaques belong to players who were briefly famous for reasons the modern file no longer explains properly. Others belonged to pitchers who accumulated two hundred wins quietly enough that nobody noticed until the retirement ceremonies had already been scheduled.

That happens more than people think. The league needs both groups. Without the anchors, the mythology collapses. Without the outer rows, the mythology starts looking suspiciously efficient.

Do not mistake the distinction between anchor and outer plaque for a distinction in worth. The archive rarely thinks emotionally about greatness. It thinks structurally. Some players become architecture. Others become population density.

A player in your universe may retire tomorrow and enter a ballot this book never mentions. That matters. The archive takes those careers seriously too. But the founding ledger exists to establish the older gravity first—the names already waiting when new universes begin generating themselves into existence.

Harlan Moon was there before your first countdown timer.

So was Pete Harrow. So was Doon Static losing one hundred nineteen games badly enough that modern audit clerks still invoke the season as shorthand during meetings. These things persist because the league decided they would persist. That may be the closest thing the MAL has to religion.

I think sometimes about the clerks whose names ended up on the cover only because somebody in the publication office eventually has to sign the compilation paperwork. I did not play baseball. Most of the people who shaped this league did not. They filed things. They corrected things. They argued over missing road games and radio provenance and attendance totals written in fading pencil.

Marta Glass probably mattered more to the survival of the archive than half the men with plaques.

The league understands this occasionally. Usually by accident.

If you found this volume through the League page, through a small link buried somewhere near the standings or the history tab, then welcome. You are not expected to memorize the extension tier. You are not required to decide whether Len Casket or Rocco Dream belongs higher in the mythology. The archive itself never fully decided that either.

It simply kept both folders.

The foundries cooled. The radios stayed on. Floodlights failed. Audit clerks certified impossible summers with forms stamped at two in the morning. Children learned Moon and Harrow before they learned the geography attached to them.

The file kept growing because nobody inside the league ever learned how to stop adding pages to it.

Somewhere tonight a scheduled game will finish without you watching it. A box score will be filed. Somebody will joke about the result on the Sickos feed before the final inning summary finishes posting. A player in one of your universes will gain stars. Another will lose a roster spot quietly three seasons from now and disappear into the outer rows of league history where most careers eventually settle.

The standings will update anyway.

That is probably the healthiest thing about the MakeAwesome League. It does not pause for mythology. It only files mythology afterward.

One standings page. Several contradictory explanations. A room where the coffee is stale and the filing cabinets occasionally jam in wet weather.

The schedule knows what it did.
